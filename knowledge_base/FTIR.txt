TOOL: Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy (FTIR)

WHAT IT MEASURES:
- Molecular functional groups and chemical bonding via infrared absorption fingerprinting
- Presence and concentration of specific chemical species (quantitative via Beer-Lambert law)
- Thin film composition, contamination, and oxidation state in surface or bulk samples

RESOLUTION: Spectral resolution: 0.5–8 cm-1 (standard); wavenumber range: 400–4000 cm-1 (mid-IR); spatial resolution (FTIR microscopy): ~10–20 µm

BEST FOR:
- Identifying organic contaminants, polymer species, or surface functionalization on materials
- Monitoring oxide growth, nitridation, or chemical conversion in thin films
- Quality control and purity verification of bulk powders, liquids, and polymers

NOT SUITABLE FOR:
- Homonuclear diatomic molecules (N2, O2, H2) — IR-inactive, no dipole moment change
- Sub-monolayer surface species on bulk samples (low sensitivity without ATR or IRRAS enhancement)

SAMPLE PREP:
Transmission mode: powders mixed with KBr and pressed into pellets; liquids placed between IR-transparent salt windows (NaCl, ZnSe). ATR (Attenuated Total Reflectance) mode: sample pressed directly onto ATR crystal (diamond, Ge, or ZnSe) — most convenient and requires no preparation. Thin films can be measured in transmission or reflection-absorption (IRRAS) mode on metallic substrates.

OUTPUT:
Absorption or transmittance spectra (% T or absorbance vs. wavenumber cm-1); peak identification via library matching (NIST, commercial databases); quantitative concentration from peak area calibration; difference spectra showing before/after chemical changes.

COST/AVAILABILITY:
$15–$60/hr at shared facilities; instruments cost $20K–$150K. One of the most accessible and widely available analytical techniques — found in virtually every chemistry, materials, pharmaceutical, and polymer lab worldwide.
