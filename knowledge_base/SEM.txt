TOOL: Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM)

WHAT IT MEASURES:
- Surface morphology and topography at nanometer to micrometer scale
- Elemental composition (when equipped with EDS/EDX detector)
- Grain size, particle size distribution, and surface feature dimensions

RESOLUTION: 1–20 nm spatial resolution (field emission SEM: ~1 nm; tungsten filament SEM: ~10–20 nm)

BEST FOR:
- Imaging surface features, cracks, pores, and grain boundaries in solid materials
- Failure analysis and quality control of micro/nanofabricated devices
- Particle morphology characterization (powders, nanoparticles, thin films)

NOT SUITABLE FOR:
- Imaging internal bulk structure or subsurface features (use TEM for cross-sections)
- Non-conductive samples without coating or low-vacuum mode (charging artifacts)

SAMPLE PREP:
Samples must be dry, clean, and vacuum-compatible. Non-conductive samples require sputter-coating with a thin (~5–10 nm) layer of gold, platinum, or carbon to prevent charging. Samples are mounted on aluminum stubs using conductive tape or silver paste.

OUTPUT:
Grayscale secondary electron (SE) or backscattered electron (BSE) images; EDS spectra and elemental maps showing spatial composition distribution; quantitative dimensional measurements via image analysis software.

COST/AVAILABILITY:
$50–$200/hr at university shared facilities; widely available at most research universities, national labs, and industrial R&D centers. One of the most commonly available nanoscale characterization tools.
